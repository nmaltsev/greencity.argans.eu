99% of the global population are breathing poor-quality air

According to a 2022 World Health Organisation report almost the entire global population breathe air that exceeds WHO guideline limits and contains high levels of pollutants. Since 2000, the levels of these pollutants in cities has risen significantly.

More than half of the world's population lives in cities and many of these cities share the problem of poor air quality. By 2050, 70% of the world's population will reside in urban areas, and outdoor air pollution will lead to about 6.6 million premature deaths and widespread loss of healthy years of life. Air pollution is the leading environmental health risk factor globally. The pollutants of major public health concern include fine particulate matter, nitrogen dioxide, ozone, carbon monoxide, and sulphur dioxide.

Death and ill health attributable to air pollution is now estimated to be on a par with other major global health risks. Lowering air pollution levels can reduce the incidence of disease from stroke, heart disease, lung cancer, and both chronic and acute respiratory diseases. [WHO 2021]

Air quality is closely linked to the earth's climate and ecosystems globally. Many of the drivers of air pollution (i.e. combustion of fossil fuels) are also sources of greenhouse gas emissions. Reducing air pollution levels can contribute to the near- and long-term mitigation of climate change.

Policies for a cleaner urban environment

In 2015, the World Health Assembly adopted a landmark resolution on air quality, recognising it as one of the leading avoidable cause of death and ill health. Member states pledged to tackle the problem.

The European Environment Agency 2021 Zero Action Plan sets the target to reduce the number of premature deaths due to exposure to fine particulate matter by 55% by 2030, as compared to 2005. [EEP 2021]

The European regulations for air quality (e.g., Air Quality Directive 2008/50/EC) and other environmental policies (e.g., EU Biodiversity Strategy 2030; EU Pollinators Initiative) commit territories and cities of at least 20,000 inhabitants to publish their Carbon Footprint every three years, a Territorial Climate Air and Energy Plan every five years and to develop an Urban Greening Plan.

Greener Cities, Cleaner Air

Urban reforestation, i.e., increasing the tree density in cities and peri-urban reforestation near densely populated cities, is a strategy to mitigate the air pollution and climate change effects, enhance biodiversity, and provide recreational areas.

Planting trees can help improve air quality and meet clean air standards in cities. Trees have a remarkable range of traits that can help reduce urban air pollution, and cities around the world are looking to develop planting strategies to harness the benefits of public green spaces.

Our promise to plant 3 billion additional trees is all about planting the right trees, in the right place, for the right purpose.

Virginijus Sinevičius, European Commission

To set up ambitious greening plans adapted to actual needs, city planners need a suitable selection of plant species and a realistic tree inventory (public and private) to properly assess the role of urban green spaces in mitigating air pollution and climate change effects in and around the cities.

Urban planting and re-naturing or 'greening' public spaces has a major role in reducing air pollution and improving air quality, mitigating climate change effects, enhancing biodiversity, and promoting 'wellness' by providing recreational areas and fostering social cohesion.

Trees absorb atmospheric pollutants, like sulphur dioxide and nitrogen dioxide, through their leaves and filter fine particulates by deposition on foliage, from where rain washes it into the drain. Absorbing carbon dioxide and releasing oxygen during the day green plants also enhance air quality.

Trees provide a cooling effect through creating shady spots but also by the process by which energy from the sun evaporates water that has been transpired from roots to leaves. This reduces the solar energy available for warming the air.

Urban re-forestation and renaturing provides havens for wildlife, protecting biodiversity. Green channels can link and help maintain habitats supporting and preserving threatened species and ecosystems.

Outdoor activities and social interactions are essential to human health, development and wellbeing. Public green spaces satisfy a vital role in creating safe and healthy areas of enhanced natural beauty for leisure and social activities.

Trees have a direct mitigating effect by sequestering carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and offset an urban carbon footprint. By maintaining soil stability and reducing sediment run-off trees help reduce the risk of erosion and, by replacing asphalt and concrete, increase permeability to ameliorate flooding.

Impact Assessment

Not every tree species is suitable for urban planting. Air pollution removal by trees strongly depends on the location, tree species, and structural characteristics of individual trees, such as tree height, leaf area index, and crown diameter.

To date, urban trees' air pollution removal capacity have been investigated in a few cities through municipal tree inventories, mainly based on field observations and mapped in Geographical Information Systems. Some, like the i-Tree ECO model (US Department of Agriculture), include cloud sourcing components and air quality metrics but usually these inventories include only trees managed by the municipality, which can represent as low as 20-30% of the total number of urban trees.

The main limiting factor for studying individual trees is the high cost and spatial resolution of techniques, such as aerial photography and airborne laser scanning.

Mistakes have been made, some municipalities have hurriedly planted wrong tree species, and these planting strategies have degraded air quality and increased asthma exacerbation. For example, Aspen and poplar are members of a tree genus that emits high levels of isoprene, that reacts with sunlight and NOx to produce ozone.

Isoprene

Isoprene is ubiquitous. It is the building block of terpenes and these Volatile Organic Compound (VOC) poses both a primary health risk and indirectly through photochemical reactions producing ozone and PM25 particulates, elements of smog.

Case Studies

In 2019, 29 and 69 premature deaths and 189 and 416 hospital admissions could have been "avoided" by respecting the World Health Organization annual limit values in Aix and Florence, respectively.

This was the sobering conclusion of an ARGANS Case Study of two European cities. On the other hand, by selecting efficient plant species and developing an ambitious urban greening strategy, ARGANS could provide a detailed estimate of improvements to meet the city's air quality standards.

For instance, each newly reforested area (based on 400 trees) will remove annually at least 3.0 tons of O3, 2.5t NO2, 1.5t PM10, 0.8t PM2.5, 10t CO2, and lead to an ambient air two °C cooler compared to the surrounding area and increased carbon stocks (2t per ha).

With the above air pollution reduction, two premature deaths and ten hospital admissions for respiratory and cardiovascular diseases could be "avoided" annually. Each newly reforested area could prevent damages valued at €173,000 per year due to the quantity of O3, NO2, PM and CO2 removed. The newly reforested area can also markedly improve citizens' quality of life by providing a space to meet others, play sports and participate in an active, healthy lifestyle.
